Executive Summary

The right amenity, at a budget built to pencil.

This analysis answers the question ownership asked on July 6: does the stabilized NOI of a five-treatment-room spa justify the cost of building it? On the structural assumption ownership requested, a brand new 4,000 square foot building developed on one of the resort's existing pads, the answer is yes, provided the building is delivered to a disciplined budget. At a design-to-cost budget of $2.80M all-in, the spa's roughly $302K of stabilized annual NOI produces a 10.8% yield on cost, clearing the floor of the 10% to 14% range that institutional hotel owners such as Blackstone, KSL, and Sunstone typically require of new capex projects, on the spa's four walls alone and before crediting anything else the amenity does for the resort.

Crediting what it does for the resort strengthens the case considerably. A modest, defensible $10 premium on stabilized hotel ADR, a fraction of what a true spa typically supports at this positioning, adds roughly $82K of rooms NOI and lifts the combined yield to 13.7%, comfortably inside the range. Under the upside operating case, a stronger local and member wellness business, the project runs at 15.2% on the spa alone and 19.6% with the rate premium, above the range entirely.

The discipline that makes this work is stated plainly rather than buried: $2.80M is a design-to-cost commitment, roughly $480 per square foot of hard cost on a simple, single-story building with an efficient wet core, not a loose estimate that scope can grow into. The stabilized NOI in this analysis supports an investment of roughly $3.0M on the spa's four walls and up to $3.8M once the rate premium is credited. That is the project's entire cost envelope, and the sensitivity grid in this analysis shows exactly where creep beyond it breaks the return. Held to budget, the spa clears the institutional test and completes the luxury positioning the entire five-year rate strategy depends on.

Why A Spa At All

The strategic case, independent of the math

The Amenity Expectation

At the rate positioning this plan targets, a true spa is not a differentiator. It is the cost of entry. Every luxury resort Tetherow Resort will be compared against offers one, and its absence is a visible gap in the guest journey and in every OTA and consortia listing.

The Traveler We Are Pricing For

The guest paying $320 and up per night in Bend is booking a wellness-inflected trip: golf, trails, recovery, treatment. The spa is what converts that guest at the rate we are asking, and what earns the second and third night of the stay.

A New Revenue Center

Five treatment rooms, run at established resort norms, generate roughly $1.87M of stabilized annual revenue from guests, rental program residents, members, and the Bend local market, a customer base the resort already owns and currently sends elsewhere.

Brand Standards

A dedicated spa is core to Appellation's definition of a complete luxury property. It anchors the wellness pillar of the repositioning and gives the rate strategy in the five-year pro forma a physical proof point.

The Benchmark

What a 10 to 14 percent yield on cost means

Institutional hotel owners evaluate discretionary capex projects on unlevered yield on cost, sometimes called cash on cash return or return on cost: the project's stabilized incremental NOI divided by the total cost of delivering it. Large owners such as Blackstone, KSL Capital Partners, and Sunstone typically target 10% to 14% for revenue-generating additions of this kind. The logic is simple: a new amenity carries development risk, ramp-up risk, and operating risk, so it must out-earn the roughly 7% to 9% cap rates at which stabilized resort cash flow already trades. A project inside the range creates value the day it stabilizes; a project below it destroys value relative to simply holding the money.

Yield on Cost  =  Stabilized Incremental NOI  ÷  Total Project Cost. All figures in this analysis are unlevered, before income taxes, and expressed on a stabilized Year 3 basis. No terminal value or exit multiple is assumed.

The Investment

Development budget: ground-up 4,000 SF spa building

The program: five treatment rooms, a dedicated spa reception with retail, men's and women's locker rooms, sauna and steam offerings, a relaxation area, and back-of-house support. The budget is built design-to-cost: a single-story, wood-frame, slab-on-grade building with simple massing, a tightly planned wet core to contain the most expensive square footage, and finishes that earn their luxury through design rather than material cost. It assumes the pad is served, with utilities at the lot line, and that scope is locked before pricing. Costs assume 2026 to 2027 delivery in Bend, Oregon.

The $2.80M budget sits inside what the most conservative view supports at a 10% hurdle, with roughly $220K of cushion, and comfortably inside every other view. The corollary is a hard guardrail: cost creep past roughly $3.8M pushes the project below institutional hurdles even with the rate premium credited. The budget is not a starting point for scope additions. It is the ceiling that keeps the project inside the range.

Recommended next steps

First, commission concept design with early contractor involvement and price the program to a guaranteed maximum against the $480 per square foot hard cost target before any commitment is made; the return case is only as good as the budget's enforceability. Second, validate the local and member demand assumption, the swing variable in the operating model, with a limited market study of Bend's spa supply and pricing. Third, in parallel, price one or two conversion candidates on property, such as underused back-of-house or the sales office; a conversion delivering the same five-room program below the target basis would move the project from the floor of the institutional range toward its middle without touching a single operating assumption.
